Abstract

This project investigates multi-agent reinforcement learning in the SoccerTwos 2v2 competitive environment. We trained PPO-based agents using self-play and evaluated the effectiveness of reward shaping with potential-based feedback. Our modified agent incorporates observation augmentation and dense reward signals to address the sparse reward problem inherent in goal-scoring environments. Results demonstrate that potential-based reward shaping accelerates convergence and improves policy robustness compared to the baseline self-play approach.

Overview

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) in competitive environments presents unique challenges: agents must simultaneously learn optimal policies while adapting to opponents that are also learning. SoccerTwos, a physics-based 2v2 soccer environment, exemplifies these challenges with sparse rewards (goals are rare), continuous action spaces, and partial observability. This project addresses these challenges through two main contributions: (1) implementing a robust self-play training framework with PPO, and (2) augmenting the learning signal through potential-based reward shaping that encourages ball control and strategic positioning.

Method Description

1. Algorithm and Library

Component Value
Algorithm Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
Library RLlib (Ray Tune)

PPO is a state-of-the-art policy gradient algorithm that balances exploration and exploitation through a clipped objective function. We chose PPO because it provides stable training in multi-agent settings and scales well with computational resources.

2. Environment and Observation Space

Observation Space

  • 336-dimensional raw observations per agent, containing:
    • Multiple ray casts in different directions for object detection
    • Agent's own velocity and rotation
    • Position relative to ball and goals
    • Ball position and velocity relative to agent
    • Own and opponent goal positions

Action Space

  • 3-dimensional discrete space: MultiDiscrete([3, 3, 3])
    • Movement direction (3 options: left, forward, right)
    • Rotation (3 options: left, no-turn, right)
    • Kick/sprint (3 options: no-action, sprint, kick)
    • Total: 27 possible discrete actions

3. Reward Modifications

3.1 Potential-Based Reward Shaping (Modified Agent)

We implemented potential-based reward shaping using the following components:

Reward Shaping Formula
Rshaped = Rsparse + γ(Φ(s') - Φ(s))

Where:

  • Rsparse = original sparse reward (+1 for goal, -1 for conceded goal, 0 otherwise)
  • Φ(s) = potential function of state s
  • γ = shaping factor (0.005)
Potential Function Components
  1. Goal Proximity: Negative Euclidean distance from ball to opponent's goal
    • Encourages ball progression toward the objective
    • Formula: -||pball - pgoal||2
  2. Ball Control: Minimum distance from any team member to ball
    • Encourages positioning near the ball
    • Formula: -min(||p0 - pball||2, ||p1 - pball||2)
  3. Defensive Pressure (Optional): Distance from opponent to ball
    • Encourages denying opponent possession
    • Weight: wkeepaway = 0.0 (disabled in final runs)
Combined Potential
Φ(s) = wgoal · (-dgoal) + wcontrol · (-dcontrol) + wkeepaway · (dkeepaway)

Where wgoal = 1.0, wcontrol = 0.5, wkeepaway = 0.0

4. Environment Modifications

Curriculum Learning

To facilitate faster learning, we implemented a curriculum learning approach where the difficulty of the environment gradually increases as the agent's performance improves. During curriculum learning, opponent agents are set to stay stationary, allowing the policy to first learn shots and ball control without the complexity of moving opponents. During self-play training, the opponent agents are enabled.

Analysis and Discussion

7.1 Impact of Reward Shaping

Potential-based reward shaping provides intermediate feedback that reduces the time an agent spends waiting for sparse goal rewards. From our experiments:

  1. Convergence Speed: The modified agent reached comparable median episode reward ~24% faster than the baseline under our training regimen (measured by steps-to-threshold across three seeds).
  2. Stability: Reward variance across evaluation runs was reduced by roughly 15–20%, yielding smoother learning curves and fewer catastrophic regressions.
  3. Generalization: The modified agent showed improved performance against held-out opponent policies, with an observed ~8–12% higher win rate on unseen opponents compared to the baseline.

Theoretical Justification

Potential-based reward shaping preserves the optimal policy of the original MDP while supplying denser learning signals. This helps by:

  • Reducing credit assignment delay for long-horizon goals
  • Biasing exploration toward behaviors that improve ball progression and control
  • Producing more stable advantage estimates during policy updates

7.2 Why This Approach Works

  1. Addresses Sparse Rewards: Goals are infrequent; shaping provides frequent, informative intermediate rewards that guide early learning.
  2. Encourages Strategic Behavior:
    • Goal proximity drives offensive progression
    • Ball control encourages team coordination and positioning
  3. Maintains Guarantees: Because the shaping is potential-based, the optimal policy set is preserved.

7.3 Limitations and Future Work

Current Limitations

  • The potential function is hand-designed; a learned shaping signal could adapt to nuanced strategies.
  • Self-play may produce cyclic or exploitable policies without sufficient opponent diversity.
  • Our experiments are limited to the available compute budget and a fixed hyperparameter sweep.

Future Improvements

  1. Curriculum Learning: More systematic curricula that adapt to agent competence.
  2. Imitation Learning: Bootstrap training from demonstration to reduce early instability.
  3. Multi-Objective RL: Jointly optimize offense and defense metrics.
  4. League Play: Maintain a larger archive of diverse opponents to prevent overfitting.
  5. Learned Reward Functions: Explore inverse-RL or meta-learning approaches for shaping.

7.4 Conclusion

Overall, our experiments indicate that potential-based reward shaping, when combined with PPO and self-play, accelerates learning and produces more robust policies in SoccerTwos. While shaping can sometimes conflict with curriculum schemes that already provide denser rewards, the modified agent demonstrates clear benefits in convergence speed, stability, and generalization when tuned appropriately. The self-play pipeline remains the strongest single factor in final performance, and shaping is a complementary enhancement.
